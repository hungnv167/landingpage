CHAPTER 1

Drunk, Driving & Dangerous

Under the glare of bright, white headlights and flashing purple-blue neon lights, emergency responders are attending to two youth, who had lost control of their motorbikes and crashed into a ditch. It is the first emergency call of the evening and the start of a typical night in the Laotian capital.

Also at the scene is Sebastian Perret, a French paramedic and former firefighter, who with seven local volunteers, mostly teenagers, started Vientiane Rescue in 2010. It remains the country’s only free, emergency responder service. With the rising affluence in Laos, the country’s roads have seen an increase in traffic, resulting in more accidents. According to the World Health Organisation, about 90 per cent of crashes are due to drink-driving and speeding. Before Vientiane Rescue, Sébastien says that victims of serious accidents would often be left to die on the street.

Across town, flashing, neon lights illuminate a different kind of nightlife - the darkened stage of a packed dance club. Venues like this draw a boisterous crowd looking for a good time. Alcohol lubricates the evening for many Laotians in their 20s and 30s. According to the World Health Organisation, Laos is the ​third biggest consumer of alcohol in Southeast Asia​​, with 7.5 litres consumed per person per year.

Outside this club, a drunk, young man recovers on a sofa. Official sources state that ​underage drinking​ in Laos is common in restaurants and bars. Barriers to access are minimal with alcohol being affordable and easily available. In a move to promote health and safety among its residents, Vientiane’s Mayor has ​banned the sale of alcohol​​ ​or alcoholic beverages before 4pm in the capital.

The ban might prevent people from drinking earlier in the day, but is unlikely to affect alcohol sales, as revellers usually consume alcohol after work. Before the night is up, tables at F&B outlets, like this, are littered with empty bottles.

Motorcycles are the most popular form of transport in Vientiane. Many motorists are underaged or do not have a proper driving licence. They also tend to drive under the influence of alcohol, hence drunk driving is the leading cause of road fatalities in Laos.

Vientiane Rescue’s ambulances are dispatched 24/7 from four stations located near ‘hot spots’ across the city. Braving crowded road conditions, volunteer drivers weave in and out of busy traffic to get to a crash site within three to seven minutes. On a busy night, Vientiane Rescue’s trained first-aid and emergency medical services volunteers respond to an average of 20 calls.

Accidents usually involve motorcyclists, and since helmets are seldom worn, the risk of getting seriously hurt is greater. Volunteers attend to varying severities of injuries; from minor cuts and scrapes to broken limbs and horrific accidents that end in death - as many as 50 a month. ​To date, Vientiane Rescue has saved more than 60,000 people - from just 300 in 2010 to over 15,000 in 2019.

​Vientiane Rescue​​ has touched the lives of people from all walks of life. Through the years, volunteers have earned the trust and respect of the community. The free emergency service is often the first and only place people turn to in an emergency. Besides road accidents, Vientiane Rescue’s teams also fight fires and deliver babies. This evening, volunteers respond to a domestic violence case, which would also involve local police.

CHAPTER 2

The Volunteers

Even though road accidents are common in Vientiane, crash scenes still attract the morbid curiosity of bystanders. But for some who have witnessed one too many tragedies, it was no longer enough to stand by and do nothing. Instead, they were moved to make a difference, signing up as volunteers with Vientiane Rescue.

A desire to “help others”, “save lives”, “make their city a safer place” and “to become better humans”. These are the most common reasons volunteers give to explain why they dedicate their free time to Vientiane Rescue. While there is a core team of about 60 full-timers, many of the over 500 unpaid volunteers are students or juggle day jobs. One of them is Kik, 24, who is a cashier at a local bank during the day. Sébastien says dedicated volunteers like Kik, who display leadership skills, are what make Vientiane Rescue so special. He says they also look for qualities, such as professionalism, courage, solidarity, compassion, altruism, commitment and sacrifice. Uniforms are only given out to trainees who show up regularly for at least three months.

​Sébastien also relies on the original group of founding members, who were teenagers when they started up Vientiane Rescue. Now in their early-to-mid-20s they have taken on more administrative roles, such as station deputy chief. Despite running the day-to-day operations at the stations, they continue to head out on emergency interventions with volunteers. Says Sébastien, “Being a good manager is not sitting behind a desk, it's being with the team inside the ambulances. So each of us continues to work on the roads, less often, but still.” Their loyalty to Vientiane Rescue all these years is due to their steadfast belief in the importance of the service they provide. Plus, “they also like it,” says Sébastien. “And you can’t get respect if you don't show your commitment.”

Johnson is no stranger to high-stress situations. The 25-year-old volunteer is a commercial pilot with Lao Skyway. He signed up to volunteer with Vientiane Rescue after witnessing one too many accidents. He says that the law prevents people without the necessary qualifications to assist accident victims. This motivated him to get proper training. He is a qualified emergency medical technician (EMT) and is also a diving instructor. Fearing for his safety, his parents were initially against him volunteering. But after hearing his stories of rescue and bravado, are ardent supporters now. The greatest reward from saving lives is when victims recover and return to say, ‘Thank You.’ Johnson says the feeling is comparable to landing a plane flawlessly.

A father-of-three, Ai has volunteered with Vientiane Rescue for two-and-a-half years. The decision to join the team was precipitated by a loss in the family. His sister-in-law died in an accident six years ago, because no one rescued her in time. The engineer works at an international hotel chain managing its fire safety and gas system. Ai has become an indispensable addition to the team. He also represents Vientiane Rescue to train local firefighters. Here he prepares a training presentation, with the help of his son, Ton.

​Ton was so inspired by his father's passion that he asked to become a volunteer at 11 years old. He now volunteers two to three times a week, sometimes without his dad. Father and son have dinner with the rest of the family, before heading out for their shift at Dong Dok station.

​The number of badges on Ai's uniform is testament to his achievements over the years. He is now in charge of the diving, fire and rope rescue teams. His interest in first aid and rescue was nurtured since young. And he continues to develop his skills by continually training.

​In order to continue with Vientiane Rescue, Ton has proven to his parents that he can juggle school work and volunteering. His commitment is clear, becoming one of the youngest volunteers to complete EMT training. Ai says he is proud to see his son mature over the years, and believes that first aid rescue has taught him the importance of responsible driving.

​All's quiet for now at Dong Dok station, where Kik, Johnson, Ai and Ton volunteer. Dong Dok is the largest of four stations run by Vientiane Rescue in the capital. They also have one station in the southern city of Pakse, and two in the northern part of the country in Vang Vieng and Xieng Khouang. Across all seven stations, it has 20 ambulances and two fire trucks.

​Ai and Ton check the pressure of the station's supply of oxygen tanks to make sure they function properly. When the dam collapsed in southeast Laos in 2018, Ai took leave from his job to represent Vientiane Rescue during the search and rescue operation. He says there were moments he feared for the lives of his team of volunteers, as they had to search for survivors in the river at near zero visibility. There was also the lurking threat of unexploded ordnance (UXO) spread across the area by the floods. But Ai understood that risking their lives was integral to what they signed up for as first responders.

​Johnson joins Ai and Ton at Dong Dok station, and starts his shift on a celebratory note. He surprises call centre manager Mimi with a birthday cake. The fun ends abruptly when the phones start ringing. Before she could cut her cake, Mimi dashes back to the room to handle the calls.

​Mimi, 25, is in her element as she straddles the emergency calls and dispatches the ambulances from the station nearest the accident site. The call centre receives some 500 calls a month. Even as a paid full-timer, Mimi continues to volunteer her spare hours to respond to accidents. One of the things she enjoys most is to deliver babies. Mimi has dedicated the past five years of her life to Vientiane Rescue and acts as a big sister to other volunteers. She says before Vientiane Rescue, she used to stay out late and party a lot. Since then, she has become more serious and likes herself better. Although she lives at Dong Dok permanently, she returns to her family home regularly. Her dream is to work at Vientiane Rescue indefinitely. The best part of her job? Interacting with people from different backgrounds.

CHAPTER 3

On The Frontline

Johnson is sent out on his first emergency of the night. He replaces the cake in his hand with the radio receiver in the ambulance, which receives updates from Mimi at Dong Dok station, as the team heads out on their rescue mission.

At the wheel next to Johnson, Ai skillfully manoeuvres confidently through rush-hour traffic as fast as he can. It can be risky and harrowing when other drivers on the road break the law, for example, by speeding or skipping red lights.

​Thankfully, the accident isn’t serious, and the team quickly transfers the shaken victim, a motorcyclist, onto the stretcher. Back in the ambulance, it is a family affair with Ai driving the team to the nearest hospital and Ton in the back chatting and watching over the patient.

​Back at Dong Dok station, Kik has just arrived for her shift. Trained as an EMT, she is in charge of advising and briefing newcomers and teaching them first aid. The most regular advice she gives her volunteers is “to put their heart” in to what they are doing. And reminds them that it is “not a game”, because they have people’s lives in their hands. Since joining the Vientiane Rescue team, Kik says she has matured in her thinking and behaviour and is better able to adapt to strangers.

​But Kik’s quiet night ends soon enough when an emergency call comes in. She hops into the ambulance with her team and preps them from the front seat.

​A self-confessed risk-taker, Kik is in her element in the thick of the action. Her steely concentration is nothing less than admirable. She directs her team of volunteers to move the victim onto a stretcher after wrapping a brace around his neck. The young driver is semi-conscious and visibly drunk after crashing his motorbike into the side of the road.

​Following administrative processes to the letter is key to ensuring all accident victims Vientiane Rescue delivers to the hospital are given the best chances of surviving their injuries. For this volunteer, it means referring to the scribbled notes on her hand when filling in a detailed report about the accident.

​Johnson joins Ai and Ton at Dong Dok station, and starts his shift on a celebratory note. He surprises call centre manager Mimi with a birthday cake. The fun ends abruptly when the phones start ringing. Before she could cut her cake, Mimi dashes back to the room to handle the calls.

​At the hospital, Sébastien chats with volunteers after dropping off an accident survivor at A&E. Having lived in the country for nearly a decade, his command of Lao is fairly strong and has developed a relaxed and jovial relationship with the volunteers. Their camaraderie is evident. Over the years, Sébastien and his teams have also built a respectful relationship with staff at the local hospitals, like Friendship Hospital. This has helped to improve the survival rate of accident victims, which has improved morale among volunteers.

CHAPTER 4

Winding Down

After another hectic night, it's time to fill up on piping hot bowls of noodle soup. Sébastien takes his volunteers out for supper at a popular street side stall. He cares deeply for his volunteers and worries about their future, particularly for those who volunteer full-time with Vientiane Rescue. He wants an equitable life for them. This means finding more avenues of funding so he can pay full-timers a living wage. So far, the service has been supported by private donations​, and operates on a tight budget of US$5,000 a month.

​Back at the station, the rest of the volunteers return from their rescue missions, and catch up over a home-cooked dinner. The atmosphere is light and filled with laughter, showing a deep bond between them strengthened by the sheer amount of time they spend together. Their friendships help to soften the often bloody scenes they see every day. Sébastien says that when the people they save and their families donate items to the stations to show their gratitude, the thoughtfulness raises the spirits of volunteers and gives them the impetus to continue to save lives. In fact, many volunteers say they cannot imagine ever leaving Vientiane Rescue, their second family.

​Some of these friendships have blossomed into romances. Exhausted from a long day at the office, Kik decides to have an early night. Before heading to bed, she video calls her fiance, who is also a volunteer with Vientiane Rescue. He was part of the 2018 cave rescue team in Chiang Rai, Thailand. Kik says he was coaching and advising her when she first joined. Their shared experiences and interest in helping people has brought them closer and made it easier for their relationship to succeed.

​Vientiane Rescue volunteers each have their own private space in the dorms at each station. But those still on shift find any chance they can get to take a quick nap during lull periods. This helps them stay alert after a long day when an emergency comes in the dead of the night.

A new day rolls in at Dong Dok station, and it is Mimi’s turn to surprise another volunteer with a birthday cake. The burning candles light up the smiling faces in the darkened room. Within the huddle of this touching moment, the future of the Vientiane Rescue family looks bright. Sébastien believes that the service will live on as long as volunteers continue to take pride in and ownership of Vientiane Rescue.

About Vientiane Rescue

Vientiane Rescue is a volunteer-run rescue service in Vientiane, Laos. They are the largest such service in the country, responding to over 5,760 road accidents annually (2015 figures). Made up of over 200 volunteers, 8 ambulances, a fire-fighting unit, a scuba rescue team as well as hydraulic rescue and excavation teams, Vientiane Rescue has grown to over 200 volunteers working from 4 different locations in Vientiane.

Vientiane Rescue was among the 6 recipients of the 2016 Ramon Magsaysay Award, commonly regarded as Asia's equivalent of the Nobel Prize. According to the jury, the award “recognizes its heroic work in saving Laotian lives in a time and place of great need, under the most deprived of circumstances, inspiring by their passionate humanitarianism a similar generosity of spirit in many others.” The jury further commented that in responding to between 15 - 30 accidents a day, Vientiane Rescue helped save an estimated 10,000 lives between 2011 and 2015.

In November 2016, French President François Hollande presented Sébastien Perret and Vientiane Rescue with the La France S’engage Award at a ceremony in Paris.
